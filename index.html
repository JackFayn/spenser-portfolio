About Me

My dad and I built my first computer from scratch. We spent ten days in his office piecing together parts from Amazon, Micro Center, and a donated video card from his coworker, reading manuals and watching YouTube guides whenever we hit a wall. By the end I had a gaming PC that doubled as a school computer, but the real takeaway wasn't the games. Watching those unfamiliar parts fit together like puzzle pieces and somehow produce images on a screen felt like magic. I wanted to understand how it all worked.

Building a computer is expensive, so I found cheaper ways to chase that same feeling. My dad and I set up a home media server on a Raspberry Pi, basically a homemade Netflix running off a local hard drive. That project cemented my interest in computer engineering, and it started showing up in the classes I chose. By high school my schedule was almost entirely STEM. As the math got harder I enjoyed it more, not less. The satisfaction of finally getting a difficult concept pushed me to study ahead on my own, and I ended up self-teaching through Calculus II.

That pattern has stuck with me. Challenges aren't something to avoid; they're how you actually learn a subject instead of just skimming the surface. I expect the problems only get harder from here, but the curiosity and stubbornness that got me through those ten days in my dad's office haven't gone anywhere.

What We Are Building

Our VIP team fabricates supercapacitors that will be launched to high altitudes aboard NASA's High Altitude Student Platform (HASP), a balloon-based research program sponsored by NASA and LSU Space Grant. The goal is to test how these supercapacitors perform under the extreme conditions of near-space flight.

The project is split into four sub-teams: Fabrication builds the supercapacitors using carbon nanotube (CNT) electrodes and chemical coatings. The Supercapacitor Design team works on the electrolyte materials (PVA gel mixed with KOH or ionic liquids). Systems Design handles the DAQ board and PCB that will fly on the payload. And my team, Characterization, tests the finished supercapacitors to measure their electrical properties before they go up.

My Role on the Characterization Team

I work with the Characterization sub-team, where our job is to evaluate the performance of each supercapacitor the Fabrication team produces. We run three main types of tests:

  • Cyclic Voltammetry (CV) using the Gamry instrument. This test sweeps voltage at a constant rate while measuring current. The area of the resulting CV curve tells us the average capacitance of the supercapacitor. A larger enclosed area means more charge storage.
  • Galvanic Charge Discharge (GCD) using the Arbin instrument. We hold current constant at 10 mA and measure how voltage changes over time as the supercapacitor charges and discharges. The voltage drop at the transition from charge to discharge tells us the internal resistance. We typically run 100 cycles per sample.
  • Scanning Electron Microscopy (SEM) to image the carbon nanotube forest on half-assembled supercapacitors. The SEM fires an electron beam at the sample surface and produces high-magnification images that let us check the height and uniformity of the nanotubes.

Results and Observations

We tested the F24-1 P14 supercapacitor (ionic liquid electrolyte) on the Arbin at 3.0V. The GCD curves show the expected logarithmic charge and discharge behavior: voltage climbs quickly at first as the large voltage difference drives high current, then levels off as the capacitor approaches the supply voltage. Discharge follows the inverse pattern.

Over 100 cycles, we observed that capacitance starts high (around 1.75 mF) and gradually decreases to roughly 0.5 mF. This is expected because GCD testing is destructive to the supercapacitor material. Resistance fluctuated in the 30,000 to 60,000 Ohm range. Efficiency started low (below 40%) but climbed to 70-90% within the first 10-20 cycles as the electrolyte wet the electrode surfaces and lowered resistance.

We also characterized a set of KOH-based supercapacitors made by the Design team. These did not perform well: the CV curve area was very small, indicating poor charge storage. The GCD on the KOH samples produced a square wave instead of the expected logarithmic curve, suggesting the supercapacitor was not functioning as intended.

During testing on March 3, one of the supercapacitors cracked, which we documented. This kind of structural failure is something the team needs to address before flight-ready units are produced.

Discovery Project: Arduino Temperature & Humidity Reader

Platform
Arduino Uno
Language
C++ (via the Arduino IDE)
Hardware
DHT sensor, OLED display, breadboard, jumper wires
Outcome
Working temperature and humidity monitor

Discovery Project Idea:

My initial idea for the project was that I wanted to familiarize myself with a piece of equipment that I had never worked with before, and after looking through some examples of devices used for this project I decided on the Arduino Uno. From there I searched the internet for projects that can be done with the Arduino, and considering the heat outside I decided on a temperature and humidity reader.

Project Progress:

I was able to quickly find a tutorial online that included a part listed outside of just the Arduino itself. While some of the parts needed to be substituted due to them not being in supply or were not able to be shipped here, I was fortunately able to find all the necessary parts off of Amazon. After gathering all the necessary parts, I followed along with the tutorial to code the Arduino Uno through the Arduino IDE. However the Arduino IDE is coded in C++, so I had to get a handle on the syntax to understand what I was actually coding.

Successes and Failures:

When doing this project, I experienced both success and failure. I had failed when I made a mistake in ordering the parts and bought male to female jumper wires. The DHT sensor is connected to the OLED display by a breadboard, and those two devices are connected to the Arduino by male-to-male jumper wires. Not having the proper wires mean the Arduino could not be connected to the external devices. I had another failure when I tried to connect the Arduino to my computer and found that I did not have the proper cable to connect it. This was an oversight on my part and a failure to gather the necessary materials. Fortunately, I was able to borrow the correct cable from a friend.

I didn’t only fail though; I succeeded in completing the project and having a working temperature and humidity sensor. The project gave me the confidence to be able to use a new programming language and gave me a rudimentary understanding of how to code electronic devices.

Skills Gained and Reflections:

I gained a few skills throughout the project, one of them being able to code basic C++ through the Arduino IDE. This includes the basic syntax of the programming language and particular commands that stem from the DHT sensor library. I was also able to increase my understanding of how to use a breadboard from beyond just the common electronic devices or circuit elements like logic gates and resistors. Finally, I also learned how to operate the actual Arduino itself, such as what the purpose of each port is on the Arduino.

Overall, this project was a great experience with learning skills that I would not have learned in the classroom. I can say that this project has given me an increased interest in my threads because I enjoyed working with the physical devices to create a practical effect.

The finished build. The OLED reads out room humidity, temperature, and heat index from the DHT sensor on the breadboard.
